Architecture · Operating Model

A modern data architecture rests on four pillars. Most teams build three.

Strategy says why. Architecture says how data flows. Platform is what your teams use to build and run it. Operating model decides whether any of it produces what the business asked for. The fourth is the one that breaks. And it's the one agents expose first.

01

Strategy · The Why

Strategy is the only pillar the business writes.

Strategy names the outcomes data is supposed to deliver. The questions the business needs answered. The decisions it needs to make faster. The workflows it expects to automate.

Without a strategy, every piece of analytics work is a request. Each one is judged on its own merits, not against a direction the team is moving. The other three pillars exist to deliver this one. If the strategy isn't written, the other three are running blind even when they're running well.

Open question

Can you state your data strategy in one sentence without naming a tool? If the sentence requires the words Snowflake, dbt, or Sigma to land, you have a platform brief, not a strategy.

02

Architecture · How, At The System Level

Architecture is how data moves, not what tools move it.

The technical design at the system level. Sources, layers, contracts, consumption surfaces. How a row of raw event data becomes a number on a dashboard or an answer to a query.

Architecture lives one level above the platform. It describes flow and shape — what models exist, what they depend on, what guarantees they make. Most teams confuse it with the platform, and the diagram on the slide ends up being a logo grid: Salesforce arrow Snowflake arrow dbt arrow Sigma. That diagram doesn't describe an architecture. It describes a procurement order.

Stated blind spot

Most "architecture diagrams" are platform diagrams. The architecture is what the diagram leaves unsaid — the layers, the contracts, the grain at each step, the rules nobody had to enforce because the system did.

03

Platform · The What

A platform is what you bought. It is not what you built.

The collection of tools your teams actually use to run the architecture. Warehouse, transformation, orchestration, semantic layer, BI, observability.

The easiest pillar to acquire. The easiest to mistake for the others. A team can replatform from Redshift to Snowflake or from Looker to Sigma and not change a single decision about how data work gets done. Buying a platform decides nothing about strategy. It runs no operating model on its own. Tools sit inside the architecture and inside the operating model — never above either of them.

Recommendation

Name your platform in one breath. If it takes three, you have a stack, not a platform. And if you can't name the boundary between platform and architecture, the boundary doesn't exist — the platform is doing your architecture's job, badly.

04

Operating Model · How, At The Organizational Level

The fourth pillar decides whether the first three deliver.

The operating model is the set of decisions about how data work happens. Who owns what. How requests flow. What the system enforces. What people are trusted to remember.

Strategy, architecture, and platform produce the outcomes the business named only when these decisions are made deliberately. When they aren't, the platform compensates for the operating model nobody wrote down. The team gets faster at the workaround, never at the work.

The seven decisions every operating model answers

Ownership

Accountability for each data product, each source, each incident. When something breaks, the owner is named before anyone asks.

Team topology

Central, embedded, hybrid. The hardest decision to change later, which is why it should be the first decision made.

Workflows

Request to delivery. Code promotion. Access provisioning. Repeatable, documented, identical across teams.

Standards

Naming, layers, documentation, testing, branching. Decided once, written down, enforced by something other than memory.

SLAs

What the business can expect — freshness, KPI delivery speed, recovery time. Without SLAs, every request becomes a negotiation.

Governance

Approvals, sign-offs, sensitive fields, lineage. A live dimension of every decision, not a separate project to start next quarter.

Enforcement

Whether the system enforces the above, or a human is expected to remember. The single most diagnostic question in the model.

Posture

All seven decisions get made. The choice is whether they're decided deliberately or compensated for by whoever gets there first.

The diagnostic that separates the two modes

The core issue: are the controls that matter enforced by the system, or by people following a process? That single question separates an operating model that scales from one the team is constantly working around.

Checklist control

Depends on people remembering.

  • Quality scales with onboarding thoroughness
  • Degrades under growth, turnover, and pressure
  • Audit trail is manual and drift-prone
  • Each team eventually invents its own variant
  • Failure looks like inconsistency, not breakage

Platform enforcement

The system prevents the wrong action.

  • CI/CD blocks the merge before the violation lands
  • Access provisioning is automatic and revocable
  • Audit trail is generated, not maintained
  • Standards stay identical across teams by default
  • Scales cleanly with headcount and surface area

How checklist control compounds

  • New hires inherit SOPs instead of guardrails
  • New business units multiply manual steps
  • Vendor frameworks deepen the lock-in
  • Audit drift hides because nobody's looking

Recommendation

Every standard is either enforced by the system or it isn't a standard. There is no third option. A naming convention that lives in a Confluence page is a suggestion; the same convention enforced by CI/CD is a contract. Treat the difference as load-bearing.

05

Forcing Function · Agents

Agents make the missing pillar visible.

An agent doesn't fail at strategy. It fails at the seam between strategy and operating model — what's in scope, what's authoritative, what's deprecated, who's allowed to see it.

The warehouse that worked for a careful human reviewer breaks the moment an agent browses freely. Wide tables exhaust context faster than humans worked around them. Column-level documentation moves from optional to critical path. Lineage moves from nice-to-have to a trust requirement — an agent that can't trace a number to a source can't defend the answer it just gave. Access models that assumed a single human reviewer fail under natural-language query patterns the operating model never specified.

None of this is new. The operating model gaps were always there. The agent is the user that doesn't know which workaround the team learned to use, doesn't notice the table comment that says "DEPRECATED — see v2," and doesn't ask before joining the wrong customer ID. It exposes everything the team had been compensating for.

Open question

Can your architecture support an agent that browses freely, or does it depend on a human knowing which table to trust? The honest answer to that question is the answer to whether your operating model is real or improvised.

06

Diagnostic · Six Questions

Six questions surface which pillar is missing.

Every shop has answers to these. The question is whether the answers were decided deliberately, written down, and enforced by the system — or whether they emerged by accumulation.

  1. Can you state your data strategy in one sentence without naming a tool?

    Strategy

  2. Can a new engineer derive layer, owner, and content from a table name alone?

    Architecture

  3. Can you name the boundary between your platform and your architecture in one breath?

    Platform

  4. Can you name the owner of any data product in under thirty seconds?

    Operating Model · Ownership

  5. Are the controls that matter enforced by the system, or by people following a process?

    Operating Model · Enforcement

  6. Could your team extend the framework if your systems integrator walked away tomorrow?

    Operating Model · Lock-in

The ask

Which of these can you answer cold? Which are you compensating for? Which would an agent expose first? The pillar that's missing isn't the one you can't define. It's the one you've been compensating for without naming.
